Understanding binaries

The life and death of multiple-star systems

The majority of massive stars are now known to be in binary (or higher multiplicity) systems. Interactions between companion stars, often involving transfer of Mass, alter the evolution of the individual stars and the binary as a whole. The physical processes involved in binary formation and evolution are currently uncertain; however, observations of astrophysical phenomena associated with different stages of binary evolution can illuminate our understanding. The question is: How do we translate observations into constraints on the physical processes that govern binary systems?

To solve this, we are developing Compact Object Mergers: Population Astrophysics and Statistics (COMPAS). COMPAS combines tools for statistical analysis and model selection with rapid population synthesis, allowing inferences to be made about the details of stellar and binary evolution.

COMPAS was initially created in order to explore the astrophysical implications of gravitational-wave observations . Gravitational Waves are the result of mergers of double compact objects (Neutron Stars or Black Holes), and are now detected regularly by the LIGO/Virgo Collaboration. COMPAS uses models of binary stellar evolution to make predictions of the rates and properties of the mergers, which are then validated against the observed population in order to constrain the binary physics.

COMPAS has since expanded to include other observational signatures of binary evolution, including Double Neutrons Stars, Type 1a and Pulsational Pair Instability Supernovae, Luminous Red Novae, and Single Pulsars, to name a few. Different observations supplement gravitational wave data to provide new insights into uncertain mechanisms and create a more complete picture of binary astrophysics.
